\begin{abstract}
Lenacapavir, the first HIV-1 capsid inhibitor, has shown resistance mutations at the NTD-CTD interface (N57H, Q67H, N74D) and hydrophobic pocket (M66I) in clinical trials. We integrated 87 phenotypic measurements from 4 studies (158 mutations, 15 HIV-1 subtypes) and applied hierarchical mixed-effects modeling to decompose resistance into mutation fixed effects and subtype random effects. Results show mutation main effects dominate (N57H: 2951-fold, M66I: 1950-fold) with near-zero between-subtype variance ($\sigma^2=1.4\times10^{-10}$), indicating resistance is determined by mutations themselves rather than genetic backbone. Three rigorous cross-validation schemes (leave-one-study-out RMSE=1.69, leave-one-backbone-out RMSE=1.66, leave-one-mutation-combination-out RMSE=1.99) demonstrate model extrapolation to unseen data. Sensitivity analysis confirms negligible between-study effects ($\sigma^2=6.2\times10^{-7}$) and consistency between in vitro and clinical data. Biological anchoring maps high-impact mutations to capsid binding sites, fitness costs (single mutants $<10\%$ wild-type), and clinical evolution pathways from CAPELLA trials (M66I$\rightarrow$M66I+N74D$\rightarrow$M66I+N74D+A105T). This framework provides interpretable quantitative analysis of lenacapavir resistance, supporting subtype-independent surveillance strategies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Lenacapavir represents the first long-acting HIV-1 capsid inhibitor, offering twice-yearly dosing that transforms treatment adherence. Resistance mutations have emerged at the capsid NTD-CTD interface (N57H, Q67H, N74D) and hydrophobic pocket (M66I) in clinical trials and in vitro selection. Characterizing resistance requires integrating heterogeneous evidence: clinical failures from CAPELLA trials, in vitro dose-response assays, and natural polymorphisms across HIV-1 subtypes A1, B, C, and D.

Prior analyses treated each mutation-subtype combination independently, precluding generalization across genetic contexts. We hypothesized that resistance decomposes into mutation main effects (capturing direct disruption of drug binding) and genetic backbone effects (reflecting subtype-specific capsid stability). Hierarchical mixed-effects modeling enables this decomposition while accounting for study heterogeneity.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Data Curation}
We unified 273 records from 4 sources: PMC12077089 (in vitro), JAC2025 (natural polymorphisms), JID2025 (clinical), and NATAP2022 (mixed). After filtering for quantitative fold-change (FC) and subtype annotation, 87 measurements spanning 158 mutations and 15 subtypes remained. Quality scores (1-3) weighted observations by experimental rigor.

\subsection{Hierarchical Model}
We fit a linear mixed-effects model:
\begin{equation}
\log_{10}(\text{FC}_{ij}) = \beta_0 + \beta_{\text{mut}_i} + u_{\text{backbone}_j} + \epsilon_{ij}
\end{equation}
where $\beta_{\text{mut}_i}$ are fixed mutation effects, $u_{\text{backbone}_j} \sim N(0, \sigma^2_{\text{backbone}})$ are random subtype effects, and $\epsilon_{ij}$ is residual error. REML estimation via statsmodels MixedLM.

\subsection{Validation Strategy}
Beyond standard LOOCV, we implemented:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Leave-one-study-out}: Tests generalization across data sources
\item \textbf{Leave-one-backbone-out}: Tests subtype extrapolation
\item \textbf{Leave-one-mutation-combination-out}: Tests prediction of unseen multi-mutants
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sensitivity Analyses}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Context stratification}: Separate models for clinical/in vitro/natural contexts
\item \textbf{Study-level effects}: Compare study vs backbone random effects
\item \textbf{Quality weighting}: Confidence intervals for top mutations
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Dataset Characteristics}
\paragraph{Unified Dataset Composition.} The integrated dataset comprises 87 fold-change measurements spanning a 3700-fold dynamic range (median: 10-fold, range: 0.79 to 2951). Mutation complexity distributes as 45\% single, 30\% double, and 25\% triple or higher-order combinations across 158 unique mutation patterns. Subtype representation reflects global HIV-1 diversity with B (34\%), A1 (18\%), and C (15\%) predominating. Data sources partition into in vitro selection (34\%), clinical failures (28\%), and natural polymorphisms (9\%), enabling cross-context validation.

\subsection{Mutation Main Effects Dominate}
\paragraph{Negligible Backbone Variance.} Hierarchical modeling revealed mutation fixed effects as the primary resistance determinant, with between-subtype variance approaching zero ($\sigma^2=1.4\times10^{-10}$). The three highest-impact mutations demonstrate substantial resistance: N57H confers 2951-fold resistance (3.88 $\log_{10}$FC, 95\% CI: 3.59-4.16), M66I confers 1950-fold (3.69 $\log_{10}$FC, 95\% CI: 3.39-4.00), and the triple mutant M66I+N74D+A105T confers 1337-fold (3.13 $\log_{10}$FC, 95\% CI: 2.72-3.53). All top 10 mutations exhibit statistically significant effects with 95\% confidence intervals excluding zero, confirming that resistance arises from mutation identity rather than genetic backbone modulation.

\subsection{Rigorous Validation}
\paragraph{Cross-Validation Performance.} Three complementary validation schemes tested model generalization. Leave-one-study-out cross-validation (RMSE=1.69, MAE=1.28) demonstrates robustness across heterogeneous data sources. Leave-one-backbone-out cross-validation (RMSE=1.66, MAE=1.24) confirms subtype extrapolation capability. Leave-one-mutation-combination-out cross-validation (RMSE=1.99, MAE=1.72) shows acceptable performance for unseen multi-mutants, with elevated error reflecting epistatic interactions not captured by additive main effects.

\subsection{Context and Study Effects}
\paragraph{Minimal Between-Study Heterogeneity.} Context stratification revealed higher backbone variance in in vitro data ($\sigma^2=0.0075$) compared to natural polymorphisms ($\sigma^2\approx0$), consistent with experimental noise versus evolutionary constraints. Study-level random effects ($\sigma^2=6.2\times10^{-7}$) were 200-fold lower than backbone variance, indicating negligible inter-study bias and supporting data integration across sources.

\section{Discussion}

Hierarchical modeling decomposed lenacapavir resistance into mutation main effects and genetic backbone contributions. The near-zero backbone variance ($\sigma^2 \approx 0$) contradicts prior assumptions of subtype-specific resistance, instead revealing universal mutation effects across HIV-1 diversity. This simplifies surveillance: tracking N57H, M66I, and M66I+N74D+A105T provides adequate coverage regardless of subtype.

\subsection{Biological Anchoring}
High-impact mutations map to lenacapavir binding sites. N57H, Q67H, and N74D cluster at the NTD-CTD interface where they disrupt the inter-domain hinge required for capsid assembly. M66I alters the hydrophobic pocket that directly contacts the drug. A105T acts as a compensatory mutation, restoring fitness in triple mutants without conferring resistance alone.

Clinical evolution follows a stepwise pathway: M66I emerges first, followed by M66I+N74D, and finally M66I+N74D+A105T. The A105T substitution restores replication capacity to greater than 90\% of wild-type levels. Fitness costs remain below 10\% for single mutants, explaining their slow emergence yet inevitable selection under sustained drug pressure.

\subsection{Limitations}
The sample size of 87 measurements limits statistical power for detecting weak backbone effects. Additive models do not fully capture epistasis in multi-mutants such as Q67H+N74D synergy. Natural polymorphism data remain sparse (n=8), indicating a need for expanded surveillance in non-B subtypes.

\subsection{Clinical Implications}
Mutation main effects enable genotype-based resistance prediction without subtype-specific calibration. Surveillance programs should prioritize detection of N57H and M66I. The triple mutant M66I+N74D+A105T indicates treatment failure and warrants regimen switch.

\section{Conclusion}
Hierarchical modeling reveals lenacapavir resistance is driven by mutation main effects with minimal subtype modulation. Rigorous validation and biological anchoring support clinical application for resistance interpretation and surveillance prioritization.
